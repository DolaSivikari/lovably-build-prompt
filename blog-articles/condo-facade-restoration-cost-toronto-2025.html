Cost Breakdown: What You're Paying For

Labor (40-50% of total cost):

  • Skilled trades (EIFS applicators, masons, concrete specialists)
  • Scaffolding/swing stage setup and operation
  • Project management & coordination
  • Safety compliance (Working at Heights certified crews)

Materials (30-40% of total cost):

  • Base coats, finish coats, sealants
  • Waterproofing membranes
  • Flashing and trim materials
  • Manufacturer specifications often dictate specific products

Equipment & Access (15-25% of total cost):

  • Scaffolding rental ($3-12/sq ft depending on height)
  • Swing stages or boom lifts
  • Protection systems (sidewalk canopies, debris chutes)
  • Equipment mobilization & demobilization

Engineering & Permits (5-10% of total cost):

  • Building condition assessment
  • Engineering specifications
  • Toronto building permits
  • Weekly engineering inspections

Factors That Increase Costs

1. Building Height

  • Low-rise (1-3 stories): Scaffolding ~$3-5/sq ft
  • Mid-rise (4-8 stories): Scaffolding ~$5-8/sq ft
  • High-rise (9+ stories): Swing stages ~$8-12/sq ft
  • Example: A 15-story building vs 4-story building = 40-60% higher access costs

2. Condition of Existing Facade

A "healthy" building with regular preventive maintenance requires less repair work than a "failing" building with 10+ years of deferred maintenance. We've seen condos with maintenance neglect requiring 3x more concrete repairs than similar buildings with proper upkeep.

3. Occupied Building Restrictions

  • Work during business hours only: No premium
  • Nights/weekends required: +15-25% labor costs
  • Phased work to minimize disruption: +10-15% overall cost
  • Example: Luxury condo requiring weekend-only work = $1.82M vs $1.45M quote for unrestricted access

4. Heritage Designations

Toronto heritage properties require City approval for facade changes, matching historic materials (+20-40% material costs), and specialized craftspeople (+15-30% labor costs).

5. Winter Weather

  • Ideal season (May-October): Standard rates
  • Winter work (November-April): +10-20% (heaters, enclosures, productivity loss)
  • Emergency winter repairs: +25-40%

6. Hidden Conditions

  • Unknown substrate issues discovered during demolition: Change orders +$50K-200K
  • Asbestos abatement (1970s-1990s buildings): +$15-40/sq ft
  • Structural issues found during repair: Varies widely

How to Get an Accurate Quote (Not Just a Ballpark)

Step 1: Commission a Building Condition Assessment (BCA)

What it is: Engineer inspects facade, tests materials, provides detailed report

Cost: $3,500-12,000 (depends on building size)

Deliverable: Report with repair quantities, photos, recommended scope

Why it matters: Prevents "low-ball" quotes that balloon with change orders

Step 2: Create Detailed Specifications

What it is: Engineering firm writes technical specs for bidding

Cost: $5,000-15,000 (often combined with BCA)

Deliverable: 30-50 page specification document

Why it matters: All contractors bidding on same scope = apples-to-apples comparison

Step 3: Request Itemized Quotes from 3 Contractors

What to ask for:

  • Breakdown by trade (masonry, EIFS, waterproofing, etc.)
  • Breakdown by building area (north facade, south facade, parkade, etc.)
  • Timeline with milestones
  • Payment schedule
  • Warranty terms (years, what is covered)
  • Exclusions (what is NOT included)

Red flags:

  • Lump sum quote with no breakdown
  • "We will figure out the details later"
  • No mention of permits or engineering
  • Unusually low quote (30%+ below others)

Step 4: Check References & Past Projects

What to verify:

  • Similar building type and facade system
  • Similar project size
  • WSIB clearance
  • Insurance coverage ($5M liability minimum)
  • Bonding capacity (if required)

Cost-Saving Strategies (Without Cutting Corners)

1. Preventive Maintenance

Savings: 40-60% over lifecycle

How: Annual inspections ($2K-5K/year) catch small issues before they become big problems

Example: $15K sealant repair vs. $450K water damage restoration

2. Phased Approach

Savings: 15-25% financing costs

How: Spread work over 2-3 years to match reserve fund contributions

Example: 3-phase project avoids need for $1.2M special assessment

3. Off-Season Scheduling

Savings: 10-15% labor costs

How: Schedule non-critical work in shoulder seasons (April, November)

Trade-off: Slightly longer timeline

4. Value Engineering

Savings: 10-20% without sacrificing quality

How: Engineer reviews spec, identifies equivalent lower-cost materials/methods

Example: Alternative sealant brand (same performance) saved $38K

5. Bundled Services

Savings: 8-12% vs. separate contracts

How: One contractor handles masonry + waterproofing + coatings

Benefit: Single point of responsibility, fewer coordination issues

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How often should a condo facade be restored?

A: Depends on facade type and maintenance:

  • EIFS: Recoat every 10-15 years, full restoration every 25-30 years
  • Brick: Tuckpointing every 20-30 years, major work every 50-75 years
  • Concrete: Repairs as needed (inspect every 5 years), coatings every 15-20 years

Q: Can we get government grants or rebates?

A: Limited options in Ontario:

  • CMHC Green Home program (if adding insulation): Up to $5,000/unit
  • Toronto Better Buildings Partnership (commercial): Low-interest loans
  • CHIP (Ontario): Only for energy retrofits, not cosmetic repairs

Q: Should we hire a reserve fund study company or engineer?

A: Different purposes:

  • Reserve Fund Study: Financial planning, estimates costs
  • Engineer BCA: Technical assessment, defines actual scope
  • Best practice: RFS every 3 years, Engineer BCA before major project

Q: What is the difference between "repair" and "restoration"?

A:

  • Repair: Fix specific problems (e.g., patch 500 sq ft of spalled concrete)
  • Restoration: Comprehensive overhaul (e.g., address all deficiencies, restore to "like-new")
  • Note: Restoration costs 2-4x more but lasts 2-3x longer than repairs
